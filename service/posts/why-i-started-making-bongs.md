It started as a dare, honestly. A friend asked if I could throw one on the wheel and I said "probably?" Three prototypes and a lot of trial and error later, they're now my best sellers.

The challenge with a bong — structurally — is that it's basically a vessel with a very specific water-to-air ratio requirement. Too thick at the neck and the airflow is terrible. Too thin and it'll crack during firing. I broke a lot of them.

## what i learned

The first one was a disaster. I got the shape right but didn't account for the downstem angle, so the water just sat there doing nothing. The second one was better but the bowl was too small — looked great, useless in practice.

By the third, I figured out a system. I throw the body first, leave it to firm up, then add the mouthpiece and the downstem hole while it's leather-hard. Getting the angle right on that hole is still the fiddliest part.

## the glazing problem

The other challenge is the interior. You want a smooth, non-porous surface inside — which means the interior glaze has to be perfect. I've had a few come out of the kiln with pinholing on the inside wall, which is both aesthetically bad and practically annoying.

I ended up switching to a liner glaze for the interior — same one I use for mugs — and it's been solid since.

Grandma would absolutely hate these. Which, honestly, is part of the charm.
