A mug is only as good as its handle. I've pulled thousands at this point and I still get excited when one feels just right.

There are two main ways to make a handle — pulling and extruding. I pull all of mine. Extruded handles are faster and more consistent, but they have a slightly mechanical quality to them. A pulled handle has variation that I think makes it feel better in the hand.

## how pulling works

You start with a lump of clay attached to the mug. Wet your hand, grip the clay, and pull downward repeatedly, thinning it and shaping it at the same time. The goal is a handle that's even in thickness, has a slight curve that mirrors the body of the mug, and attaches cleanly at both ends without cracking.

The tricky part is getting the proportions right. Too thick and it feels clunky. Too thin and it's fragile and can feel uncomfortable to hold — your fingers have nowhere to sit.

## the attachment problem

Handles crack at the attachment points. This is the most common failure mode, and it almost always comes down to the moisture differential between the handle and the mug body. If the mug is too dry when you attach the handle, the handle shrinks faster and pulls away as it dries.

I attach handles when the mug is leather-hard and the handle has been left to stiffen slightly but not too much. There's a window — probably about two hours depending on temperature — where the attachment is reliable.

## what makes a good handle

Hard to put into words. But when you pick it up and your fingers just settle into it, when the weight feels balanced, when you don't have to think about how you're holding it — that's a good handle.

I keep a few mugs I made early on to remember how bad the handles used to be. Progress is real, even if it's slow.
