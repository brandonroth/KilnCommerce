The burnt orange I use on almost everything? That was a mistake. Mixed the wrong ratio, loaded the kiln anyway because I was tired, and pulled out the most beautiful color I've ever seen.

I'd been trying to get a terracotta-adjacent glaze for a while. Everything I tested came out either too red, too brown, or too matte. Then one night I mixed up a batch with roughly double the iron oxide I intended — misread my own notes — and fired it without testing because I was behind schedule.

The result was this deep, warm, slightly variegated orange that breaks to almost copper at the edges. I've been using it ever since and I genuinely could not reproduce the mistake if I tried. Fortunately I had the presence of mind to write down what I actually did (as opposed to what I meant to do) before I forgot.

## my current lineup

Right now I'm working with about eight glazes regularly:

- **liner white** — goes on everything functional, smooth and food safe
- **celadon** — my workhorse, pools nicely in texture
- **burnt orange** — the happy accident, used on about 40% of my work
- **matte black** — dries very matte, interesting with texture
- **ash green** — still experimenting, unpredictable
- **clear** — for pieces where I want the clay body to show
- **cobalt blue** — reserved for specific pieces, quite intense
- **rust red** — similar territory to the orange but drier

## on mixing your own

I started mixing my own glazes about a year ago. Before that I was using commercial glazes, which are fine, but you lose a lot of control. Mixing your own means you can tweak one variable at a time and actually understand what's happening.

The downside is that it's more work and things go wrong more often. Worth it, though.
